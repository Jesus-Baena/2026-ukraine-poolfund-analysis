## Section 1: Strategic Statement

Through  this  allocation,  the  UHF  aims  to  support  comprehensive,  vulnerability-focused,  and  needs-driven  multisectoral  assistance  to  newly  placed  IDPs,  returnees  and  people  living  within  frontline  areas  and  areas  affected  by ongoing shelling, aligning with prioritized activities under the 2025 Humanitarian Needs and Response Plan (HNRP). Priority must be given to extremely vulnerable individuals and locations of highest severity - please see below.

In particular, the allocation targets support and essential services for 1) vulnerable newly displaced individuals from frontline areas, 2) people living in frontline areas and areas affected by ongoing shelling, and 3) strengthening the capacity  of  smaller  Women's  Rights  Organizations  (WROs),  organizations  of  people  with  disabilities  (OPDs),  and organizations representing Roma and LGBTIQ+ communities.

The allocation will reinforce the UHF's continued commitment to localization by fostering the participation and capacity strengthening of Ukrainian organizations, including national and local NGOs, Civil Society Organizations (CSOs), and volunteer groups capable of delivering principled, context-specific last-mile assistance, particularly in hard-to-reach areas.    Specifically,  the  allocation  will  support  local  women's  rights  organizations  and  organizations  supporting marginalized groups under strategic objective 3.

## Section 2: Humanitarian Context

Since the escalation of the war in February 2022, an estimated 3.7 million people remain internally displaced, with over 78,000 of the most vulnerable housed in nearly 1,800 collective sites across 24 oblasts, constituting around 2 per cent of the displaced population.

In 2024, hostilities intensified significantly, with the length of the front line in Ukraine expanding by 15 per cent, notably in  northern  Kharkivska,  while  the  intensity  of  violence  doubled  in  Sumska  and  Chernihivska  oblasts.  Oblasts  like Donetska, Kharkivska and Zaporizka have been severely impacted, particularly in front line raions such as Bakhmutskyi, Pokrovskyi, Kupianskyi and Polohivskyi, which experienced widespread airstrikes and artillery attacks. Between May and  November,  the  most  significant  incidents  impacting  civilians  and  leading  to  mass  evacuations  occurred  in Kharkivska, Donetska, and Sumska oblasts. In Donetska oblast, authorities evacuated families with children from over 40 towns and villages between August and September, significantly reducing front-line town Pokrovsk's population from 50,000 to 10,000.

With expanded Government-led evacuation mandates, more people continue to be evacuated and displaced from and within  the  east  and  north.   However,  some  of  the  most  vulnerable  people,  including  children,  single  female  care providers,  older  people  and  those  with  disabilities,  have  remained  in  areas  with  active  hostilities.  Meanwhile,  an

estimated 12.5 million people who were not displaced from their homes, but are directly affected by the war, are among the most vulnerable with heightened needs due to limited access to services. This includes those who return to their places of origin. Although these people are spread throughout Ukraine, the highest concentration is in front-line regions and areas along the border with Russia.

Humanitarian needs in Ukraine remain critical, affecting civilians in every region. Compared to 2024, extreme and catastrophic levels of need persist in front-line areas, while extreme needs have expanded within northern areas due to the intense impact of the intensified hostilities. Intersectoral catastrophic conditions are occurring in Donetska, Dnipropetrovska,  Kharkivska,  Khersonska,  Luhanska,  Zaporizka,  with  sector  specific  catastrophic  conditions  also occurring  in  Cherkaska,  Mykolaivska,  and  Sumska. Overall,  there  has  been  an  average  increase  in  the  severity  of humanitarian conditions across all regions, except  for  Kyiv  City  and  Kyivska  oblast,  where  there  has  been  a  small reduction in the overall severity of needs. It should be recognized that vulnerable groups are more likely to face higher severity levels, often with multiple overlapping needs.

The number of people in need (PiN) of humanitarian assistance is estimated to be 12.7 million, including 2 million children, with 2.8 million internally displaced people in need of assistance and 9.9 million people who are non-displaced but have been more acutely affected by the war, including those who have been previously displaced and returned to their homes. The concentration of PiN remains high in the oblasts in the east, north, and south regions.

In anticipation of continued hostilities in 2025, the impact of the war is expected to continue and further exacerbate the  humanitarian  situation  and  correlated  needs,  with  subsequent  displacement,  damage  to  critical  civilian infrastructure, and disruption of essential services. The battle for territorial control is characterized by escalations and intensified hostilities, which creates a fluid situation. This will likely lead to new waves of displacement and mandatory evacuations, particularly in the front-line oblasts, such as Donetska, Kharkivska, and Zaporizka.

To respond to the identified needs, humanitarian organizations in Ukraine under the 2025 HNRP will aim to support 6 million internally displaced people and non-displaced war-affected people, including returnees - with two Strategic Objectives (SOs):

- SO1: Life-saving Emergency Assistance.
- SO2: Access to Prioritized Essential Services.

## Section 3: Allocation Objective and Thematic Priorities

This allocation will support the Ukraine 2025 HNRP through response prioritization guided by rigorous inter-sectoral severity analysis, supported by the 2024 MSNA data, other cluster-specific and thematic assessments, as well as the 2024 HNRP mid-year response and gap analysis, and complement the Government's response. Priorities have been formulated in consultation with partners and the ICCG.

The overall objective of the allocation is to provide comprehensive vulnerability-focused and needs-driven multisectoral humanitarian assistance to vulnerable IDPs, returnees, and other conflict-affected people in Ukraine.

The allocation will focus on three thematic priorities to address the most pressing humanitarian needs of vulnerable people in areas where the severity levels are the highest:

## 1. Support to vulnerable newly displaced people from frontline areas.

Under this priority, the UHF will fund a multi-sectoral "Response Package" for newly displaced people from frontline areas. UHF partners will provide evacuation support and limited time-bound assistance in collective sites in receiving areas as designated by the government of Ukraine or receiving high numbers of self-evacuees. Response in collective sites in oblasts most impacted by new evacuee arrivals in frontline and border areas, will be complemented by a critical package of support services for evacuees in collective sites in West and Central Ukraine, in line with the agreed ICCG strategy.

## 2. Provide prioritized essential services for people living within frontline areas and areas affected by ongoing shelling.

Under this priority, partners should provide multi-sector humanitarian assistance to address needs as a result of  the  direct  impact  of  the  ongoing  war  and  allow  for  more  flexible  programming  to  respond  to  new emergencies. Activities should enable access to services through existing national mechanisms to the extent possible,  while  complementing  Ukraine's  shock-responsive  social  protection  system,  adhering  to  the 'Reinforce, Do Not Replace' principle outlined in the Agenda for Humanity.

3. Strengthen  the  capacity  of  smaller  Women's  Rights  Organizations  (WROs),  organizations  of  People  with Disabilities (OPDs), and organizations representing Roma and LGBTIQ+ communities.

Under  this  priority,  partners  should  seek  to  strengthen  the  capacity  of  smaller  local  Women's  Rights Organizations  (WROs),  organizations  of  people  with  disabilities,  and  organizations  representing  Roma  and LGBTQI+ populations, in order to expand the pool of UHF-eligible Ukrainian organizations with expertise in providing gender-responsive and inclusive humanitarian assistance.

## Section 3.1: Strategic Focus

This allocation aims to support the following strategic focus in partner programming to achieve the above thematic priorities:

- Reaching the most vulnerable people , including IDPs, returnees, and other conflict-affected people with severe, compound humanitarian  needs,  in  particular  persons  with  disabilities,  older  people,  vulnerable  women  and children,  marginalized  groups,  including  Roma  and  LGBTIQ+  communities,  and  people  affected  by  chronic conditions.
- Localization: Direct  submissions  from  eligible  national  NGOs,  including  women-led  and  women  rights organizations,  and  submissions  from  eligible  international  NGOs  with  sub-implementing  partnerships  with national and local partners, including CSOs and volunteer groups providing last-mile humanitarian assistance in targeted communities, will be prioritized. For the latter, the main partners leading the submission should include appropriate  capacity  support  for  the  sub-implementing  partners  as  well  as  monitoring  activities  for  quality control and risk assurance in the project log frame and budget these activities accordingly.

## The UHF promotes the following good practices in local partnerships:

- o Tailored and well-timed capacity-building support to local partners;
- o Mandatory  provision  of  duty  of  care  package  to  front  line  responders,  including  first  aid  and  security training, mental and psychosocial support (MHPSS), personal protective equipment for operations in highrisk areas, and life and accident insurance;
- o Mandatory fair sharing of PSC with local partners based on their operational coverage;
- o Sufficient sub-grant duration to allow local partners' organizational continuity and development (six months or longer is recommended);
- o Innovations promoting strategic and effective localization; and
- o Making funding to local partners as direct as possible (no sub-sub-granting).

In the event that the main partner sub-contracts national or international NGOs, CSOs, CBOs or volunteer groups (Vs), the partnership arrangements and sub-grants shall be clearly spelled out in the project proposal. The main partner shall be fully responsible for all work performed by such sub-contractors and for all acts and omissions committed by them, their employees, or other personnel engaged by them.

- Disability  inclusion: Projects  demonstrating  how  the  project  aims  to  reduce  discrimination  and  barriers  for persons with disabilities to fully engage with and benefit from the response (including needs assessments, log

frame, monitoring, and accountability mechanisms with appropriate resources budgeted) will be prioritized. The inclusion of one or more disability standard indicators in the log frame is strongly encouraged.

- Protection Mainstreaming: All projects should incorporate protection principles and promote meaningful access, safety,  and  dignity  in  humanitarian  aid,  including  the  prevention,  mitigation  and  response  to  gender-based violence. Partners are required to specifically mention how protection mainstreaming will be integrated into their proposed response. Partners are encouraged to use Protection Cluster and AoRs tools to facilitate referral of people in need of protection assistance using Minimum Standards for Individual Protection Referrals and Service Advisor mapping platform to identify relevant service providers. Additionally, the use of Child Protection AoR referral pathways and CPIMS+ for child protection case management is strongly encouraged.
- Gender and age considerations : Specific needs of different gender and age groups and response to address them should be articulated in the project design and implementation plan (including needs assessments, log frame, monitoring, and accountability mechanisms with appropriate resources budgeted). Partners are encouraged to include the needs of children, according to the Minimum Standards for Child Protection in Humanitarian Actions (2019 edition) in their proposals. All projects must include the Gender and Age Marker as per IASC guidelines annexed.
- Accountability to affected populations (AAP): Projects demonstrating the involvement of affected people in all phases  of  the  project  cycle  and  accessibility  of  collective  feedback  and  complaints  mechanisms  for  affected people across gender, age, disability, and other diversity factors, in line with the 2022 Ukraine AAP Action Plan, will be prioritized. The project log frame should include at least one AAP standard indicator.
- Prevention of sexual exploitation and abuse (PSEA): Projects  demonstrating that the partner (and any subimplementing  partners)  has  mechanisms  in  place  to  prevent,  detect,  report,  and  manage  incidents  of  SEA, including assistance to survivors, in line with the 2024 Ukraine PSEA Action Plan, will be prioritized. The project log frame should include at least one PSEA standard indicator.

## Section 3.2: Multi-Sector Priorities and Parameters

The allocation will promote the following thematic priorities, aligning with prioritized activities under the forthcoming 2025 HNRP. Partners are strongly encouraged to carefully review the below and consult relevant clusters when designing project proposals.

## Allocation Priority One - Support to vulnerable newly displaced people from frontline areas

The ongoing hostilities in frontline areas have left the elderly, children and families in highly vulnerable positions and are causing widespread displacement. The most acute challenges persist for the evacuation of people with disabilities and low mobility, older people, and chronically ill people who rely almost exclusively on humanitarian actors' support for evacuations and access to specialized services upon arrival to reception areas.

Most partners have limited resources to provide the required, specialized support en route, and are thus unable to prioritize the most vulnerable groups for evacuation.  Very limited specialized accommodation basic service options to host these highly vulnerable individuals and groups in the receiving regions is highlighted in the Ministry of Social Policy's Strategy on evacuating older people and people with disabilities from institutions in the frontline areas.

The UHF, under priority one, will fund a multi-sectoral "Response Package" for newly displaced people from frontline areas. UHF partners will provide evacuation support and limited time-bound assistance in collective sites in receiving areas as designated by the government of Ukraine or receiving high numbers of self-evacuees. Recognizing the high vulnerability  of  evacuees  relocated  from  frontline  and  border  areas,  response  in  collective  sites  in  oblasts  most impacted by new evacuee arrivals in frontline and border areas will be complemented by a critical package of support services in collective sites receiving highly vulnerable evacuees in West and Central Ukraine, in line with the ICCG strategy.

Where possible and appropriate, partners should consider the provision of multi-purpose cash (MPCA) to meet the needs of targeted vulnerable groups. Where the use of MPCA is not feasible, the ICCG has agreed an emergency package of support to meet these needs that includes the most prioritized activities from the 2025 HNRP.

## Allocation  Priority  Two -Provide prioritized essential services for people living within frontline areas and  areas affected by ongoing shelling.

In 2024, hostilities intensified significantly, with the length of front line in Ukraine expanding by 15 per cent, notably in northern Kharkivska and the intensity of violence doubling in Sumska and Chernihivska oblasts. Oblasts like Donetska, Kharkivska a nd Zaporizka have been severely impacted, particularly in front line raions such as Bakhmutskyi, Pokrovskyi, Kupianskyi and Polohivskyi, which experienced widespread airstrikes and artillery attacks.

Critical infrastructure across the front -line areas in Ukraine has been massively damaged or destroyed, with 27 per cent of electricity supply stations, 25 per cent of water facilities, 42 per cent of health -care facilities and 58 per cent of educational  facilities  impacted.  Additionally,  systematic  attacks  impacting  transport  and  water  infrastructure  have disrupted supply chains, reducing access to essential goods and services in many areas. Health and education services are also severely impacted. By mid2024, 827 healthcare facilities were fully or partially damaged and about 13 per cent of health facilities had faced significant disruptions in power, water or heating.

Under this priority partners should provide multi -sector humanitarian assistance to address needs as result of direct impact of the ongoing war and allow for more flexible programming to respond to new emergencies. Activities should enable access to services through existing national mechanisms to the extent possible, while complementing Ukraine's shockresponsive social protection system, adhering to the 'Reinforce, Do Not Replace' principle outlined in the Agenda for Humanity, mainstream protection and apply risk mitigation measures to promote Do-No-Harm approach.

As under the above priority one, where possible and appropriate partners should consider the provision of multi -purpose cash (MPCA) to meet the basic needs of targeted vulnerable groups. Where the use of MPCA is not feasible, partners shall consider other activities part of the ICCG -agreed emergency package of support to meet these needs that includes the most prioritized activities from the 2025 HNRP. This may include complementarity cash assistance to meet needs outside basic needs (ex: CVA for health, pr otection, GBV survivors and those at risk, rental support).

## Allocation Priority 3

Capacity development is a key component of the UHF's Localization approach and a critical element of its support to local actors. In the context of Ukraine where smaller CSOs are leading the response along the front lines UHF has been increasing  support  to  smaller  CSOs  and  volunteer  organizations.  Building  on  lessons  learned  and  best  practices developed under previous allocations that supported local CSOs along the front lines, the UHF under this allocation will prioritize support to smaller rights-based organizations.

UHF partners under this priority should aim to strengthen the capacity of smaller local Women's Rights Organizations (WRO's), organizations of people with disabilities, and organizations representing Roma and LGBTI Q + populations, in order to expand the pool of UHFeligible Ukrainian organizations with expertise in providing gender -responsive and inclusive humanitarian assistance.

## Section 4: Complementarity

This Standard Allocation will complement ongoing UHF projects where relevant and ensure operational coordination and  programmatic  synergies  among  the  partners  funded.  Further,  this  allocation  will  complement  other  donor initiatives  supporting  the  HNRP  and  complementary  programming  for  transition  to  recovery  to  avoid  overlaps  in assistance and maximize their collective impact.

## Section 5: Project Selection Criteria

In addition to supporting the above-mentioned strategic and operational priorities, all projects to be funded through this allocation should pursue the following cross-cutting principles and key programmatic considerations.

Access and operational capacity :  Only submissions by partners with demonstrated operational presence/access in identified areas of high severity and capacity to absorb the allocated funds and implement prioritized activities in line with the sectoral technical requirements will be recommended for the Strategic Review. For this, partners should have consulted and reported their activities to relevant clusters before submitting a proposal under this allocation.

Evidence-based  approach: Needs  are  clearly identified and  articulated and  supported  by  beneficiary data disaggregated  by  gender,  age  group,  type,  disability,  and  other  factors  contributing  to  the  need  for  specialized assistance.  Partners  aiming  to  cover  hard-to-reach  areas  with  limited  needs  data  should  include  in  the  proposed response planned assessments and relevant justification, in consultation with Clusters.

Cost effectiveness: The budget is fair, proportionate in relation to the context. Proposals demonstrating better cost effectiveness will be prioritized, where: a) for comparable activities and outputs, the total cost is less; b) cost per assisted person is competitive and within an acceptable range for the cluster (based on activity-based costing and cluster-specific standard costs); c) the level of support and personnel costs is reasonable and in line with a given type of implementation modality; and d) the proposed period of implementation is adequate and represents best use of resources at that time.

Monitoring: A realistic  monitoring and reporting plan is incorporated in the project to measure the achievements against  planned  outputs  and  timeline  and  the  quality  of  assistance  provided,  including  through  sub-implementing partners.

Risk management: Assumptions and risks are comprehensively and clearly spelled out, along with risk mitigation and management strategies.

Coordination  and  complementarity: Demonstrating  the  partner's  coordination  with  relevant  clusters  and  key stakeholders  (including  local  authorities  and  other  humanitarian  partners  in  the  project  location),  as  well  as complementarity between the proposed project and activities implemented with other funding sources.

## Section 6. Eligibility criteria for this Allocation

## Partner Eligibility

- Partners planning to submit proposals under this allocation must notify and seek guidance from Clusters/Working Group Coordinators of the sectoral components of their planned projects. Projects from partners who have not notified the relevant clusters will not advance to the pre-strategic review stage. The current list of the UHF-eligible partners is located here.
- Only  current  UHF-eligible  partners  can  apply  for  direct  funding,  while  non-assessed  partners  may  be  subimplementing partners in submissions led by an eligible partner.
- To align with localization efforts and the Humanitarian Coordinator's endorsement, the UHF funding is to be prioritized for national partners, excluding direct allocations to UN agencies.

- Partners with their Due Diligence (DD) page unapproved will not be able to see the open allocation in OneGMS. Eligible partners planning to submit a proposal under this allocation are requested to submit their DD swiftly for HFU review and approval.

- Partners must perform due diligence on suppliers who participate in their procurement processes to ensure that they are not subject to a successful criminal prosecution in the past 5 years. Partners who have identified suppliers involved in confirmed incidents of corruption or fraud are kindly requested to share this information with UHF by emailing ocha-uhf@un.org and keeping nduta@un.org and roman.oleksiienko@un.org in copy. This information  will  contribute  to  forming  a  public  database  of  suppliers  which  should  not  be  used  in  projects implemented through UHF funding. The UHF Excluded Suppliers Database (available under the link) is updated and shared on a regular basis.

## 7.1 Allocation Strategy Development Process

The  allocation  round  uses  the  standard  allocation  modality,  with  the  strategic  scope  and  prioritization  criteria developed based on the 2025 HNRP.
